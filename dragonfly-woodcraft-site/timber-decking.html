The Case For Timber

Why Timber Decking Still Leads

Timber has been used in outdoor construction for centuries — not because there was nothing better, but because when it's the right species and properly installed, it genuinely outperforms the alternatives on value, character, and versatility.

Modern pressure treatment and responsibly sourced hardwoods mean a well-built timber deck will last fifteen to twenty years with basic maintenance. It ages naturally, feels warm underfoot in summer, and looks exactly like what it is: a material that belongs in a garden.

It's also the most workable material — it cuts cleanly, fits around awkward garden shapes, integrates with other garden features, and can be repaired board by board if something goes wrong rather than requiring full replacement.

  • Cost — typically 30–50% lower upfront than premium composite
  • Natural aesthetics — warm underfoot, ages with genuine character
  • Repairability — individual boards can be replaced without touching the rest
  • Workability — fits complex layouts and integrates with other garden features more easily
  • FSC-certified sourcing — all timber from responsibly managed forests
  • Proven longevity — correctly installed and maintained, 15–20 year lifespan
Honest Maintenance Reality

What Timber Actually Needs

Timber decking requires periodic oiling or treatment — roughly every one to two years depending on species and exposure. This is the main trade-off against composite.

If you're comfortable with that (many people enjoy it as part of seasonal garden prep), timber is an excellent choice. If you want to clean it once and forget it, composite is likely the better fit.

I'll tell you which makes more sense for your situation — not which one I'd prefer to install.

Species Guide

Choosing The Right Timber

Species selection matters more than most people realise. The wrong timber in the wrong situation underperforms badly. Here's what I recommend and why.

Hardwood
Premium · 20+ Years

Species like Ipe, Cumaru and Garapa are exceptionally dense and durable. They resist rot, insects and weathering without treatment. Higher upfront cost but outstanding longevity. All sourced from FSC-certified merchants.

Treated Softwood
Value · 15+ Years

Pressure-treated pine or larch is the most common choice — cost-effective, widely available, and very capable when installed correctly with the right fixings and ventilation. Requires periodic re-treatment.

Siberian Larch
Character · 15–20 Years

A popular mid-ground choice. Naturally higher in resin than other softwoods, which gives it better natural rot resistance. Weathers to a silver-grey beautifully, or can be oiled to maintain the golden tone.

Scope Of Work

What's Included

From a simple ground-level platform to a raised deck with balustrade, integrated steps and a pergola overhead — I design and build the full thing.

  • Ground-level decks — simple, clean platforms for seating areas and BBQ spaces
  • Raised decks — with correct sub-frame, ventilation and drainage built in

  • Pergolas — designed to fit the deck and the garden, not just the nearest standard size
  • Edging & framing — finishing details that separate a properly built deck from a DIY job
How It's Built

The Build Standard

Every deck I build starts with ground preparation — proper weed suppression, gravel bed where needed, and a sub-frame designed to drain correctly and stay stable. No shortcuts that show up three years later.

Posts are set at the correct depth for the load. Fixings are stainless steel or hot-dip galvanised — nothing that will rust and stain the boards. Ventilation is designed in from the start, not an afterthought.

The result is a deck that doesn't bounce, doesn't flex on the joists, and doesn't develop problems that cost as much as the original build to put right.

Most residential decks don't require planning permission under Permitted Development Rights — provided the deck is no higher than 300mm above ground, covers no more than 50% of the garden area, and is not in a Conservation Area or on a listed property. I'll flag any concerns during the site visit. For anything borderline, I recommend a quick check with South Gloucestershire or Bristol City Council before we start.
