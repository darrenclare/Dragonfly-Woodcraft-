Sourcing Standard

What FSC-Certified Actually Means

The Forest Stewardship Council (FSC) is the global certification standard for responsibly managed forests. FSC-certified timber comes from forests where harvesting rates don't exceed what the forest can regenerate, where wildlife habitats are protected, and where the rights of local communities and workers are upheld.

It's the most widely recognised and rigorously audited timber certification in the world. It's also the one that actually means something — unlike vague claims about "sustainable sourcing" with no third-party verification behind them.

Every merchant I buy from holds FSC Chain of Custody certification. That means the paper trail runs from the forest to the merchant to my van to your garden. If you want to see it, I'll show you the paperwork.

Why It Matters

Timber Without Certification Is A Gamble

Timber without FSC certification could come from legally logged but poorly managed forests, from regions where enforcement is weak, or in rare cases from illegal sources that have entered the supply chain through third-country processing.

FSC certification eliminates that uncertainty. It's not a premium add-on — it's a baseline standard, and it's the only standard I work to.

The Bigger Picture

Built Right Is The
Sustainable Choice

Responsible sourcing matters. But the most environmentally significant decision in any garden build is how long it lasts.

A deck built correctly — with the right sub-frame, the right fixings, the right drainage, and the right timber — will last fifteen to twenty years. A cheap job that fails in five means every material gets thrown away and replaced. Double the timber, double the concrete, double the plastic fixings, double the fuel to the tip. Double the cost to you, too.

That's why I don't cut corners on sub-frames that nobody will see, or use cheap fixings that rust through in three years, or skip the drainage details that make a deck last. Building it right the first time is the greenest thing I can do.

01

Right materials for the conditions

Correct species or composite product for the garden's exposure, orientation, and use. Not the cheapest option — the right one.

02

Correct sub-frame construction

Proper depth, joist spacing, ventilation, and drainage. The details that determine whether a deck lasts five years or twenty.

03

Stainless or hot-dip fixings only

Cheap fixings rust, stain, and fail. Stainless steel or hot-dip galvanised fixings outlast the boards they hold.

04

No shortcuts on ground preparation

Weed suppression, gravel beds, post depth — done properly from the start so nothing moves, heaves, or deteriorates prematurely.
